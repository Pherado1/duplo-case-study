Why VCG Started Evaluating Duplo

VCG was not looking for a new payment platform because payments were failing. The company could already pay suppliers. The real problem was everything that happened before and after the payment.

Approvals were happening on WhatsApp
Approval rules existed but were not followed consistently. Different teams used different methods. Escalations often happened over chat or phone with no paper trail.
Supporting documents were sitting in email threads
Invoices, approvals, and support documents were spread across several systems and chat channels. Reconstructing a full payment history often meant manual detective work.
Finance was always chasing people for missing information
At month-end, the team often had to piece together payment history from different places — different providers, email threads, and WhatsApp messages.
Growth was making things more complex
Operating across Nigeria, Ghana, Kenya, and the UK meant the process that worked at a smaller scale was increasingly fragile under multi-entity complexity.
The CFO's diagnosis
"The issue is not whether we can pay vendors. It is whether we can confidently explain how and why those payments happened."

This was not a payments problem. It was a governance problem.

My Onboarding Goal

My goal was not to get more logins. My goal was not just to finish setup. My goal was to make Duplo the trusted place for high-value vendor payments.

Day 60 target
High-value vendor payments could only move forward if the right approvals and supporting documents were already inside the workflow.

If that happened, governance, visibility, and auditability would improve naturally. Technology adoption was the result. Behaviour change was the goal.

Rollout Strategy: The Controlled Pilot

Instead of rolling out to the whole company at once, I designed a controlled pilot focused on the smallest footprint that could generate real signal.

Pilot scope

One procurement team. Three high-volume vendors. One approval workflow.

Bayo's procurement team became the pilot group. The selected vendors represented a meaningful part of payment activity, but were still small enough to manage closely.

The idea was simple: if Procurement adopted the workflow, Finance would have real transactions to review and validate. This was not just a user onboarding exercise. It was an attempt to change the path money took through the organisation.

Execution

The Six-Phase Onboarding Plan

Phase 1
Understand the Real Workflow
I started with process observation sessions. Instead of relying on written procedures, I asked Nkechi and her team to walk me through recent vendor payments from start to finish. This revealed what people actually did, common workarounds, where delays happened, and where the process was bypassed. The goal was to understand reality before trying to change it.
Phase 2
Build the Easiest Path
People rarely leave familiar workflows just because they attended training. They change when the new workflow is easier. I worked with Tunde and Adaeze to make raising, reviewing, and approving payments inside Duplo feel simpler than chasing approvals through scattered channels. I showed Nkechi how to use bulk-upload features and basic templates. The goal was to reduce friction, not increase pressure.
Phase 3
Activate the Key People
I did not start with broad training. Instead I focused on the people who controlled payment flow. Bayo and his main managers received targeted onboarding centred on one behaviour: approving payments inside the workflow. Bayo was not just a user — he was a gatekeeper. If he used the process, his team would follow. If he kept approving outside the workflow, adoption would stay weak.
Phase 4
Use Executive Support to Reinforce Change
The CFO (Chinedu) became a key adoption partner. His role was not daily usage — it was to reinforce expectations. Whenever there was confusion about ownership, scope, or process compliance, leadership helped bring clarity. Executive support turned onboarding from a software rollout into a company-wide change effort.
Phase 5
Watch for Workflow Leakage
I also tracked what was happening outside the workflow. Each week, Finance reviewed emergency payments, approvals made outside the workflow, missing documents, and vendor requests that were not tracked. This gave visibility into adoption risks before they turned into bigger behaviour problems.
Phase 6
Handle Urgent Requests Without Weakening Control
Urgency is one of the biggest reasons people bypass systems. Instead of ignoring that reality, the customer and I agreed on an escalation path for urgent payments — staying compliant while still responding quickly. The goal was to stay on-platform even under pressure, so urgency never became an excuse for bypassing governance.

Five Success Paths

Each path targeted a specific behaviour and carried a specific signal I could observe and measure. The paths ran concurrently — not sequentially.

1
Gatekeeper Activation
Focused only on Bayo and the three highest-volume vendors. I cut off the old payment path for those vendors and moved them into Duplo. I tracked the total number of invoices expected from each vendor each week — so I knew exactly how many transactions must appear in the workflow.
Signal: Were the expected invoices appearing in Duplo?
2
Live Onboarding Behaviour
I used a live onboarding session where the procurement team set up access in the room, in real time. I didn't want users setting up accounts privately and slipping back into old habits.
Signal: Zero delay in activation. If someone couldn't sign in before the session ended, the rollout was still fragile.
3
WhatsApp Deflection
Bayo changed his WhatsApp status and auto-reply so that approval requests for the pilot vendors could not be handled outside the platform. Every time someone tried to bypass the workflow, the request was pushed back into Duplo.
Signal: How often were WhatsApp attempts redirected? Did that number eventually fall to zero?
4
Sandbox Leakage
Every Friday, Finance (Nkechi) checked whether any money had left the business for pilot vendors without appearing in Duplo. If that happened, it meant the workflow had been bypassed. The signal was not usage — it was leakage.
Signal: Any payment to pilot vendors without a corresponding Duplo record.
5
Pressure Valve
Nkechi and I set up a dedicated "Express Bypass Workflow" inside Duplo for operations emergencies — like a container stuck at the Lagos port. This workflow stripped away minor administrative fields and required only two things: a PDF invoice upload and a single tap from a designated executive.
Signal: How fast was the executive signature added? Was the platform fast enough to handle real business pressure?

Results

Outcome: 60 Days

Controlled pilot — Vantage Consumer Group on Duplo

45→95% Invoice compliance
−70% Off-workflow approvals
3→1 day Approval turnaround
✓ Expansion approved

Approval requests for pilot vendors increasingly started inside Duplo instead of through WhatsApp or email. Invoice attachment compliance improved from about 45% to 95%. Off-workflow approval requests dropped by about 70%. Approval turnaround time fell from an average of three days to one day for pilot transactions.

Finance also spent much less time rebuilding approval history during payment reviews because the supporting documents and approval records were now in one workflow.

Most importantly, the customer showed real behaviour change. The organisation moved away from informal approval habits and toward a more controlled process with clearer accountability, stronger auditability, and better visibility into how money moved across the business. Based on the pilot results, leadership approved planning for expansion beyond the first vendor group.

Key Lesson

"Technology adoption was the result. Behaviour change was the goal."

The real work was not getting users into a platform. The real work was understanding how the customer moved money, finding where governance broke down, and helping people adopt a process they trusted enough to use by default.

That is the approach I would bring to onboarding customers at Duplo.
